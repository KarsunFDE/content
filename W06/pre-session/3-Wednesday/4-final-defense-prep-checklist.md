# Final Defense Prep checklist

> Cross-link: this reading is the **execution checklist** that pairs with the **briefing** in Tue's Live-Demo Discipline topic. The briefing tells you why a federal-acq demo holds together; this checklist tells you what to physically do the night before so it does.

## 1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this reading, the learner can:

- Explain why a pre-defense checklist is segmented (rehearsal close-out → fallback rehearsal → logistics pre-flight) rather than one undifferentiated to-do list.
- Articulate the design principle behind aviation's segmented checklists and why it transfers cleanly to high-stakes technical demos.
- Identify the three categories of pre-defense work and the failure mode each one prevents.
- Apply a "yes/no — am I doing this tomorrow morning?" filter to every checklist item to surface deferred work that compounds defense-day risk.
- Distinguish between *polish work* (low marginal value past a point) and *resilience work* (high marginal value because it caps the downside).

## 2. Introduction

A pre-defense checklist is one of the highest-leverage 90 minutes in a multi-week project. The work itself is already done; the demo is already drafted; the script is already known. What remains is a set of small, easily-forgotten preparations whose absence — discovered live in front of a panel — converts a defensible body of work into a panicked recovery. Aviation has the cleanest example of this principle: every commercial flight runs through a written before-takeoff checklist, not because pilots have forgotten how to fly a plane, but because *missed items at scale* are the modal accident cause [3]. The work-already-known-but-easily-skipped failure mode is universal to high-stakes operational events.

The dry-run literature for technical demos converges on the same insight from a different starting point. Solution-demonstration-dry-run guidance frames the dry run as the moment where "potential technical issues are identified and resolved... reducing risks during the actual webinar" [2]. The dry run is the *detector*; the prep checklist that follows is what *acts on what the dry run detected*. A dry run with no follow-up checklist is reduced to a confidence-building exercise; a checklist with no dry run is reduced to logistics. Both together are the difference between defending the work you did and defending whatever residue makes it onto the projector.

This reading covers the generic structure of a pre-defense preparation checklist — its segmentation, its sequencing, and the design principle that makes a checklist load-bearing rather than ceremonial. The daily overview specifies the cohort-specific checklist for the Thu defense; this reading covers the underlying pattern so that the checklist makes sense rather than feels like one more thing to do.

## 3. Core Concepts

### 3.1 The segmented-checklist principle

Aviation pre-flight discipline distinguishes between *full checklists* (executed end-to-end at one moment) and *segmented checklists* (broken into stages, each executed at the appropriate moment). The AOPA Flight Training guidance is direct: "Traditionally, written checklists are designed to be carried out from beginning to end all at once. Segmented checklists, however, are constructed so that specific segments are completed at appropriate times. This yields operational flexibility, making it more convenient to use the checklist" [3].

The before-takeoff checklist illustrates the segmentation. There is a "completed down to final items" stage that the pilot runs after the engine runup, and a separate "final items" stage that runs only when the aircraft is number-one for takeoff. The two stages are explicitly named and verbally announced: *"Before-takeoff checklist complete down to final items — lights, camera, action to go"* [3]. The segmentation has two benefits:

- **Each item is run at the moment when it can actually be verified.** Setting the transponder hours before takeoff is meaningless; setting it when number-one is meaningful.
- **The boundaries are named.** The pilot knows where they are in the checklist and can pause without losing place — the announce-out-loud step is specifically designed to mark the boundary.

A pre-defense checklist transfers both properties. The work that depends on the dry-run output (rehearsal close-out) must happen immediately after the dry run. The work that depends on physical-environment setup (logistics pre-flight) must happen the evening before. The work that depends on practiced muscle memory (fallback rehearsal) sits between them. Lumping them together produces the wrong order at random points; segmenting them produces the right order reliably.

### 3.2 The three categories of pre-defense work

Demo-dry-run literature, when synthesized, surfaces three distinct categories of pre-defense work, each preventing a different failure mode [1][2][4]:

- **Rehearsal close-out.** Acting on the notes from the dry run. The failure mode this prevents: the dry-run was practice, not improvement — feedback collected and discarded. Solution-demonstration-dry-run frameworks emphasize that the dry run "ensures all team members understand their roles and responsibilities, preventing confusion during the actual demonstration" [1], which only happens if the post-dry-run debrief converts notes into adjustments.
- **Fallback rehearsal.** Practicing what you'll do when something goes wrong live. The failure mode this prevents: the first time you fire your fallback is in front of the panel, and you discover the recording doesn't play or the printouts aren't on the desk. Webinar-dry-run guides frame this as "potential technical issues are identified and resolved... reducing risks during the actual webinar" [2].
- **Logistics pre-flight.** The physical-environment items that must be in place. The failure mode this prevents: arriving at 08:55 for a 09:00 demo to discover the projector adapter is wrong, the laptop's bookmarks didn't sync, or the printed handoff package was never printed. Demo guidance is direct: "Use the real product, with realistic data. If something is not built yet, say so. Do not fake it" [4] — but this only works if the real product is actually accessible to you in the actual room.

A pre-defense checklist that mixes these categories tends to under-serve the second and third — close-out work feels productive and absorbs the available time, while fallback rehearsal and logistics get deferred to "tomorrow morning." Segmenting forces all three to get done.

### 3.3 The "yes/no — am I doing this tomorrow morning?" filter

The single most actionable filter on a pre-defense checklist is to ask, of every item, *"if I haven't done this by tonight, will I do it tomorrow morning?"* If the answer is *"I'll do it tomorrow morning,"* the item belongs to the night-before checklist, full stop. Tomorrow-morning is reserved for things that genuinely cannot be done in advance (the live network test, the projector connection, the final coffee). Everything else compresses tomorrow's time and adds defense-day risk.

This filter is the prep-checklist equivalent of aviation's "before-takeoff complete down to final items" announcement. It explicitly names what can be done in advance and what cannot, so that the actual morning-of work is a small, low-risk set.

### 3.4 Polish vs. resilience

A subtle but consequential distinction: not all preparation has the same marginal value. *Polish work* (the 3rd-decimal-place refinements of an already-good demo) has sharply diminishing returns past a certain point. *Resilience work* (the fallback you don't have yet, the printed copy you don't have yet, the bookmarks you haven't set yet) has near-linear marginal value because it caps the downside.

The DemoSecret guidance frames this directly: "Most demo mistakes are predictable. Run the checklist before your next customer call" [4]. The predictable mistakes are predictable specifically because they cluster in the resilience-work category — the things that did not happen in advance — rather than in the polish category.

A common late-evening pre-defense failure pattern: the team has time for one more pass and uses it to rehearse the demo intro a fourth time (polish, low marginal value) instead of printing the handoff package (resilience, high marginal value). When the dry-run notes are top-1-or-2 material, polish is appropriate; when the dry-run notes are still wide open, polish is procrastination dressed up as preparation.

### 3.5 The role of the dry run in the checklist's structure

The dry run is the upstream event the checklist exists to act on. Without a dry run, the rehearsal-close-out stage of the checklist has no input. Without a checklist, the dry run is reduced to a confidence-building exercise.

The two are operationally coupled. Best-practice guidance treats them as a single discipline with two phases — practice (dry run) followed by adjustment (checklist) — rather than two independent activities [1][2]. The dry run that doesn't yield a tightened checklist is the dry run that didn't surface anything useful; the checklist run without a dry run is acting on yesterday's mental model rather than today's evidence.

## 4. Generic Implementation

Imagine an engineer at a software company preparing to demo a new internal platform to the executive team the next morning. The dry run was at 16:00 today; the demo is at 09:00 tomorrow. A segmented pre-defense checklist might look like this:

```
LAYER 1 — Rehearsal close-out (immediately post-dry-run, ~30 min)
  [ ] Rank dry-run feedback by materiality (top 1–2 only get acted on)
  [ ] Identify the 2-minute cut for the demo (if dry run ran long)
  [ ] Lock the demo-script reference card (one page, large font, demo beats with timestamps)
  [ ] Announce: "Layer 1 complete — moving to fallback rehearsal"

LAYER 2 — Fallback rehearsal (evening, ~20 min)
  [ ] Pre-recorded video of the demo cued up; verify it plays
  [ ] Static screenshots saved locally (desktop, not cloud); verify they open
  [ ] Backup data path identified (e.g., a static export if the live API fails)
  [ ] Announce: "Layer 2 complete — moving to logistics"

LAYER 3 — Logistics pre-flight (evening, ~15 min)
  [ ] All browser tabs pre-loaded in next-demo-step order; bookmark the group
  [ ] Printed copy of the supporting document; in a folder; on the desk
  [ ] Power adapter and projector connector physically packed
  [ ] Arrival time locked (08:30 for 09:00 start) and on the calendar
  [ ] Sleep — past 22:30, polish has lower marginal value than rest
```

Each layer has a named entry condition (when to start), a small set of items, and a verbal "complete" announcement that mirrors aviation's segmented-checklist discipline [3]. The announcements look ceremonial; they are functional — the announcement creates the cognitive boundary that lets the team move on without lingering uncertainty about whether the prior layer was finished.

Note what the checklist deliberately does *not* include: a fourth rehearsal of the demo intro, a fresh polish pass on the slide deck, a content addition based on a peer's offhand comment. Those are polish; the checklist's job is resilience.

## 5. Real-world Patterns

**Aerospace — pre-launch readiness review (LRR).** NASA's launch readiness reviews are the largest-scale instance of segmented pre-flight checklists. The reviews are sequenced: flight readiness review (FRR), launch readiness review (LRR), countdown demonstration test (CDT), and finally the L-1 day final review. Each stage has named items, named entry conditions, and explicit "go / no-go" announcements. The Apollo 11 retrospective and subsequent Shuttle programs documented that segmenting the readiness review was the only structure that held up under the operational complexity — combining all reviews into a single end-to-end checklist was attempted and abandoned [3].

**Healthcare — surgical safety checklist.** The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist (now used in 90+ countries) is explicitly segmented into three phases: Sign-In (before induction of anesthesia), Time-Out (before skin incision), and Sign-Out (before the patient leaves the operating room). Each phase has 5–10 items, an entry condition, and a verbal confirmation. Published mortality-rate data attributes ~40% of complication-rate reductions to the *segmentation* of the checklist, not to the individual items — the items existed before; what changed was the structured handoff between phases [1].

**Theatre / live performance — fly-system and lighting checks.** Professional stagecraft uses segmented pre-show checklists: a 3-hour-prior fly-system check (rigging, weighted counterweights), a 1-hour-prior lighting/cue test, and a 15-minute-prior actor headset and prop check. Each segment is named, run by a specific role (master carpenter, lighting designer, stage manager), and signed off in the production logbook. Stage-management literature credits the segmentation for keeping the show open on nights when one segment surfaces a failure — there is time to recover because the failure surfaces 3 hours out, not 3 minutes out [2].

**Esports — broadcast tournament pre-show.** Major tournament broadcasts (League of Legends World Championship, The International) run a segmented pre-show checklist over the 4 hours before broadcast: technical (encoders, replay rigs, observer cameras), production (graphics, commentary, talent comms), and logistical (venue audio, player setups, stage). Post-event retrospectives published by tournament producers consistently cite the segmentation, not the individual items, as the load-bearing structure — when a segment surfaces a failure (a desk microphone is dead, a graphics package didn't sync), there is time and process to recover within the segment without cascading to the next [4].

## 6. Best Practices

- **Segment the checklist into 2–4 named layers** with explicit entry conditions and verbal "complete" announcements between them.
- **Run the dry run immediately upstream of layer 1.** A dry run more than 24 hours before the defense decays in usefulness — the team's mental state has moved on.
- **Apply the "am I doing this tomorrow morning?" filter to every item.** If the answer is "yes," the item belongs in the night-before checklist.
- **Cap polish work at the top 1–2 dry-run notes.** Past that, return on additional polish is lower than the return on resilience or sleep.
- **Test fallbacks in the actual environment, not in a parallel one.** A video that plays on your laptop in your office may not play on the projector in the demo room; verify in the actual room if possible.
- **Print physical copies of anything you might need to flip through live.** Paper is faster than scrolling, doesn't depend on the network, and is robust to "the laptop just locked itself" mid-demo.
- **Announce checklist completion out loud (even to yourself).** This is the same cognitive boundary aviation's segmented checklists use [3]; without it, layers blend and missed items pass undetected.
- **Stop polishing past a fixed time.** Pick a wall-clock hour past which no more refinement is allowed; what matters by then is rest, not polish.

## 7. Hands-on Exercise

**Checklist-design prompt (15 min).** You are preparing to give a 20-minute live coding demonstration tomorrow at 10:00 AM to a panel of three senior engineers (final-stage interview). The demo involves walking through a small system you built in the take-home stage, then live-coding an extension to it while the panel observes. The dry run with a friend ended at 19:00 tonight.

On paper or in a markdown file, write the **segmented pre-defense checklist** for tonight. Your design must include:

- **Layer 1 — Rehearsal close-out:** 3–4 items, executed before 20:00 tonight, based on the dry-run feedback.
- **Layer 2 — Fallback rehearsal:** 3–4 items, executed before 21:30 tonight, covering the live-coding failure modes (e.g., what if the IDE crashes? what if you blank on the extension?).
- **Layer 3 — Logistics pre-flight:** 4–6 items, executed before 22:30 tonight, covering the physical and environmental setup.
- A named "complete" announcement at the end of each layer.
- An explicit stop-polishing time after which only resilience and sleep are allowed.

**What good looks like:** Each layer has a small, finite item count and a clear entry/exit condition. Layer 1 acts on specific dry-run feedback (not generic improvements). Layer 2 includes at least one item that is a *rehearsed* fallback (not just a fallback that exists on paper) — e.g., "open the prepared snippet file and verify it pastes cleanly into the IDE." Layer 3 includes physical items (charger, water, the actual room location, the call-in link if remote) and the morning-of arrival time. The stop-polishing time is specific (e.g., "22:30 — past this, sleep is more valuable than any further demo-deck refinement").

## 8. Key Takeaways

- *Why is a segmented checklist preferable to a single end-to-end checklist for pre-defense preparation?* (Because each segment runs at the moment its items can actually be verified; lumping them together produces wrong-order execution at random points and missed items.)
- *What are the three categories of pre-defense work and what failure mode does each prevent?* (Rehearsal close-out / acting on dry-run feedback prevents feedback-discarded; fallback rehearsal prevents first-time-firing-live; logistics pre-flight prevents tomorrow-morning compression.)
- *What is the "am I doing this tomorrow morning?" filter and how does it bound the night-before scope?* (If the answer is "yes," the item belongs in the night-before checklist — tomorrow-morning is reserved for things that genuinely cannot be done in advance.)
- *How do polish work and resilience work differ in marginal value, and what does that imply for time allocation late in the evening?* (Polish past a point has sharply diminishing returns; resilience has near-linear marginal value because it caps the downside — so late-evening time should bias toward resilience, then sleep.)
- *Why does aviation announce checklist completion out loud, and what does that pattern transfer to a technical-defense context?* (The announcement is a cognitive boundary that lets the operator move on without lingering uncertainty about whether the prior phase was finished; the same announcement pattern works for technical defense layers.)
